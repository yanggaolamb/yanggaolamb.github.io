  CV

Welcome! I am a final year Ph.D. candidate in Economics at the University of California, Santa Barbara. My research interests lie in Macroeconomics, Environmental Economics, and Firm Dynamics. I am on the job market in the 2025-26 academic year.

My research investigates the macroeconomic and welfare implications of climate change and climate policies. I particularly examine how climate policies interact with production-side distortions and frictions, aiming to uncover their true efficiency, distributional and aggregate impacts. My methodological approach combines econometric analysis of micro- and macro-level data with structural macroeconomic modeling.

Job Market Paper

Beyond Emissions: The Implications of Capital Misallocation on Optimal Carbon Tax
(Solo Authored)

Abstract: I study optimal carbon taxation when capital is misallocated across firms. Using U.S. public firm data, I document that firm-level returns to capital are negatively correlated with revenue-based emissions intensity, indicating that capital is misallocated toward dirtier firms. Embedding this pattern in a misallocation framework, I demonstrate a novel mechanism: a carbon tax can reduce resource misallocation and boost aggregate productivity by reallocating capital from dirtier to cleaner firms, generating welfare improvements beyond internalizing emissions. I build a dynamic climate-economy model with heterogeneous firms and tangibility-based collateral constraints: dirtier firms tend to have more tangible assets and looser borrowing limits, attracting excess capital. Calibrated to firm balance-sheet and emissions data, the model implies a carbon tax boosts aggregate productivity across a broad range of tax rates. This reallocation benefit raises the optimal carbon tax by 75% relative to a benchmark without pre-existing misallocation. Consistent with the same mechanism, removing financial frictions to equalize returns further lowers economy-wide emissions intensity.
